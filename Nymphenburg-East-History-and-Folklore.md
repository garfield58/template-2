# East of Nymphenburg Palace and Along the Canal

## A Deep History and Folklore of Western Munich — From the Ice Age to the Modern City

*A research synthesis covering roughly a 3 km radius east of Nymphenburg Palace (≈48.158°N, 11.503°E), Munich, Germany: the palace and its eastern park, the canal axis running toward the city, and the districts of Neuhausen, Gern and the Hirschgarten. Compiled June 2026 from six parallel research streams. Documented history is distinguished throughout from legend, folklore, and modern esoteric overlay.*

---

## How to read this report

The story of this small patch of ground is unusually deep because three things overlap here:

1. **A glacial landscape** that literally gave its name to the last Ice Age of the Alps (the *Würm* glaciation).
2. **A belt of ancient villages** — Neuhausen, Gern, Pasing, Allach, Menzing, Moosach — first written down in the 8th–12th centuries but settled far earlier, with some of the largest early-Bavarian cemeteries in the region.
3. **A Baroque royal landscape** — the Wittelsbach summer residence of Nymphenburg, its 50 km canal network, its hunting parks and its rich crust of court legend and ghost-lore — laid over the top from 1664 onward.

The report runs broadly chronologically, then gathers the folklore into its own section. Every major claim is sourced; uncertain or legendary material is labelled.

> **A note on sources and method.** Six research agents worked in parallel, each in English and German, across Bavarian Palace Administration material, the Historisches Lexikon Bayerns, German Wikipedia, MünchenWiki, stadtgeschichte-muenchen.de, the Ökumenisches Heiligenlexikon, and printed folklore collections (Schöppner, Schinzel-Penth, Süßmuth). In this environment direct page fetches were frequently blocked (HTTP 403), so much rests on search-engine extracts of those same authoritative pages, cross-checked across multiple sources. Points that could not be firmly corroborated are flagged. A full source list closes each section, with a consolidated bibliography at the end.

# PART I — THE DEEP PAST

## 1. The Ice Age that bears this river's name (c. 115,000–10,000 BP)

The landscape's deepest identity is glacial. The last great Alpine cold stage — the **Würm-Kaltzeit (Würm glaciation)**, c. **115,000–10,000 years ago** — was named by the geographers **Albrecht Penck and Eduard Brückner (1901–09)** after the **Würm river**, the very river that, a few kilometres west of Nymphenburg, would later be tapped to fill the palace canals. It is the youngest and best-preserved of the Alpine glaciations because nothing later overran its moraines.

The ice mass responsible was the **Isar-Loisach Glacier**, a piedmont glacier whose tongues gouged out the basins of the Starnberger See (up to ~150 m deep) and the Isar valley. As the ice melted, meltwater swept Alpine gravel northward and laid it down as the **Münchner Schotterebene (Munich gravel plain)** — the thin, permeable gravel-over-groundwater on which Nymphenburg and all the villages east of it sit. In the low spots the water table breaks surface as marsh — the *Moos* — which is the second key to the human story: easy fords and mill-power along the rivers, but poor, wet, late-drained ground on the flats.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Würm-Kaltzeit · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Würm_glaciation · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isar-Loisach-Gletscher · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Münchner_Schotterebene · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Schotterebene

## 2. The Würm river itself

The **Würm** is a 39.5 km river, the sole outflow of the **Starnberger See** (historically the *Würmsee*). It flows north through Munich's western districts — **Pasing, Obermenzing, Untermenzing, Allach** — and joins the Amper at Dachau. Its name is ancient: first attested as *Uuirma* in **772 AD**, traditionally glossed as "the swift-flowing one."

Its gradient made it a working mill-river for a thousand years — the **Pippinger Mühle**, the 15th-century **Inselmühle** at Untermenzing, the **Allacher Mühle** (first mentioned 1517) — and its water was the raw material of the Baroque canal scheme (Part III).

*Sources:* de.m.wikipedia.org/wiki/Würm_(Amper) · bfvm.de/wuerm · starnbergersee.de/sehenswuerdigkeiten/wuerm.html · stadt.muenchen.de/infos/zur-allacher-muehle.html

## 3. Prehistoric people: Bronze Age mounds and a gold-inlaid Celtic sword

Human presence on this gravel plain reaches deep into prehistory — and here it is **archaeology, not legend**:

- **Bronze Age burial mounds (Hügelgräber)**, from c. 2000 BC, were excavated around **Pasing** (southeast of the cemetery and in the Forstkolonie). For regional scale, the nearby Garchinger Heide mounds span c. 1800–1000 BC.
- The most striking Iron Age find is the **Celtic warrior's grave at Allach**, recovered during 1891 railway works by the gravel pit at Allach station. It belongs to the early/middle **La Tène period (c. 5th–4th c. BC)**, and its iron sword carries **gold inlays** on both faces — a large golden point, smaller points, a golden edge-line and a golden crescent, read as Celtic power-symbols (new moon / stylised torque).
- A remarkable detail of place-continuity: the Bavarians, some **~850 years later**, buried their own dead at the *very same Allach spot*, the Celtic grave long forgotten.
- **Celtic fortifications** near Langwied and in the Aubinger Lohe, with Roman coin finds, point to continuity from the Iron Age into Roman times.

*Sources:* dorfschreiber-obermenzing.de/pasing-geschichte.html · municharttogo.zikg.eu/items/show/192 · reise-zikaden.de (Garchinger Heide) · deacademic.com/dic.nsf/dewiki/110274

## 4. The Romans passed to the south

A useful corrective to local romanticism: the famous Roman trunk road, the **Via Julia (Augsburg–Salzburg)**, did **not** cross the Nymphenburg area. It ran **south of modern Munich** through **Gauting**, the Roman road-station **BRATANANIUM** of the Tabula Peutingeriana — a *vicus* of merchants and craftsmen with hypocaust heating and painted plaster, flourishing c. 15 BC to the late 2nd century AD.

The nearest ancient through-route in the immediate study area was a **northern salt road** (later the medieval Reichenhall/Salzburg–Augsburg *Salzstraße*) via **Langwied** — whose name, *lancwate* (c. 1100), means "long ford," marking a crossing of the marshy ground. At **Pasing**, Roman coins, lamps and cremation burials show the settlement had some significance, though the popular claim of a "Roman fort" under the later Würm-island castle is **unconfirmed**.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bratananium · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Via_Julia · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Salzstraße · dorfschreiber-obermenzing.de/pasing-geschichte.html

## 5. The Bavarians arrive: the row-grave cemeteries (5th–7th c. AD)

After Rome withdrew, the **Bajuwaren (Baiuvarii)** settled the gravel plain, and their **row-grave cemeteries (Reihengräber)** — dead laid on their backs, heads west, facing the sunrise — are the richest archaeological signature of the area. Two of the largest in the whole Munich region lie here:

- **Aubing** — a field of the 5th–7th century with **862 graves / 881 individuals** (later totals ~960), discovered 1938 during gravel extraction. A small wooden building at its eastern edge may be a very early Christian chapel — possibly **the earliest church in the Munich city area** if built around 700.
- **Allach–Untermenzing** — the **second-largest** in the Munich area: **357 graves**, c. 580–730, i.e. *before* the first documentary mentions of these villages. The Bavarians deliberately built it over an existing Bronze Age mound — and over the forgotten Celtic warrior's grave.
- **Pasing** — a 2016 excavation on Josef-Retzer-Straße uncovered a 6th-century field (the full cemetery estimated at ~900 graves) with a horse skeleton, sword, spear, shield, riding spurs and 344 amber and glass beads — the literal "Ur-Pasing."

The place-names mirror this horizon: **"-ing"** names (Pasing, Menzing, Aubing) mark the early Bavarian land-taking by kin-groups; **"-hausen"** (Neuhausen, "at the new houses") and **"-lach/-loh"** (Allach < *Ahaloh*, "forest by water") mark the settlement and clearance phases.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geschichte_Aubings · municharttogo.zikg.eu/items/show/156 & /192 · muenchen.info/ba/23/geschichte.htm · abendzeitung-muenchen.de (Pasing 2016)

## 6. The villages enter the written record (8th–12th c.)

Almost all the first mentions are in the **Freising** episcopal tradition-notices, centuries before Munich itself (1158):

The land was overwhelmingly **church property** — held by the **Hochstift Freising**, and the monasteries of **Wessobrunn, Schäftlarn, Tegernsee and Ebersberg** (the last owning mills in Moosach in the 11th–12th c.). On a Würm loop at Obermenzing a tower stood by the 12th/13th century — the nucleus of **Schloss Blutenburg**, later a ducal Wittelsbach hunting seat and the scene of the tragic Agnes Bernauer legend.

# PART II — THE VILLAGE OF NEUHAUSEN AND ITS SAINT

## 7. "Neuhausen begins with a farm"

As one source neatly puts it, *"the history of Nymphenburg begins with a castle, while Neuhausen begins with a farm — Neuhausen came first."* For roughly **seven centuries** Neuhausen remained a small farming-and-crafts village: **as late as 1839 it had barely 400 inhabitants**. A **great fire in 1794** destroyed the old village core, which was rebuilt around a deliberately widened street — today's **Winthirstraße**, a scar still legible in the street plan. The Gothic parish church (choir from the late 15th century) integrated the grave of the medieval preacher Winthir in **1597**.

*Sources:* muenchen.de/stadtteile/neuhausen · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuhausen-Nymphenburg · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winthirkirche

## 8. The Blessed Winthir — the area's oldest and most beloved legend

**[LEGEND/FOLKLORE with a historical core]** — This is the defining story of the ground east of the palace, and it exists *only* in folk tradition; there are no written records of his life.

By tradition, **Winthir** came to Neuhausen at the **beginning of the 8th century** as a wandering folk-preacher — some versions make him a nobleman, or even **an Englishman**. He earned his bread humbly as a **Säumer (muleteer)**, leading a **pack-mule laden with salt** along the old roads. When the day's hauling was done he would **sit with the farmers and speak of God**, gently Christianising the country between the Würm and the Isar. In old age he withdrew to a small house, living on vegetables he grew himself. He was credited with **miraculous healings** of people and cattle, and was buried — the tradition insisted — **beneath a linden tree**.

Folk memory held that **Neuhausen was spared in the War of the Spanish Succession** — unlike nearby Sendling with its 1705 "Mordweihnacht" massacre — because the villagers had sheltered under Winthir's protection. In **1738 the farmers of Neuhausen put their devotion in writing**, attesting that the Blessed Winthir had helped them through "numerous illnesses." He remains a protector against cattle-plagues; his feast is **29 December**.

**Then folklore met the spade.** When a **storm in 1931** collapsed part of the Winthir church, **excavations in 1932** beneath it uncovered **the skeleton of an unusually tall man together with a tree-stump** — exactly matching the legend of burial beneath the linden. The bones, judged roughly a thousand years old, have since been regarded as the relics of the Blessed Winthir.

His cult is everywhere in the modern cityscape: the **Winthirkirche**, the **Winthirfriedhof** (cemetery first documented 1315, among Munich's oldest), Winthirplatz and Winthirstraße, and the **Winthirbrunnen** on **Rotkreuzplatz** (rebuilt 1955 after wartime destruction) showing **the Blessed Winthir seated on a kneeling mule, raising a cross**. The dating is genuinely contested — the Heiligenlexikon notes the tradition splits between the 8th and the 12th century.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winthir · heiligenlexikon.de/BiographienW/Winthir_von_Neuhausen.htm · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Winthirbrunnen · encyclopedia.com (Winthir, St) · sagen.at (*Die Geschichte vom Winthirus*)

## 9. Industrial Neuhausen, the Kasernenviertel, and Gern the artists' colony

The 19th century transformed the village. As Munich grew westward, Neuhausen's population **exploded from ~400 (1839) to ~11,500 by 1890**, the year it was **incorporated into Munich**. It became the **"Eisenbahnerviertel"** (railwaymen's quarter): Georg Krauss founded his **locomotive factory in 1866** on the nearby Marsfeld, and the railway's Centralwerkstätte at Donnersbergerbrücke drove the boom. In **1876** the railway built Munich's first company housing here — the red-brick **"Rote Häuser"** (demolished 1971).

The eastern fringe became the **Kasernenviertel** (barracks quarter): the 18th-century "Neuhauser Heide" had been the garrison parade ground, and from 1860 the **Max-II-Kaserne** (3,000 soldiers) was the city's largest. Most of this military and industrial fabric was destroyed in WWII (Munich suffered 73 air raids, ~45% of its building stock lost).

**Rotkreuzplatz** — the social heart, nicknamed the "Neuhauser Stachus" and, in the 1930s, "Rio" — was named in **1903** after the Red Cross hospital built there (opened 1892). Electric trams arrived in **1900**.

**Gern**, the older hamlet of four farms (etymology: Middle High German *Gêr*, a wedge-shaped parcel of land), was reborn from **1892** when the builder **Jakob Heilmann** (firm Heilmann & Littmann) laid out the **"Familienhäuser-Colonie Nymphenburg-Gern"** — Munich's first middle-class terraced-house colony, around Böcklinstraße. Of ~280 planned houses, nearly **50 were studio-villas for artists**; around 1900 some **80 painters, sculptors and writers** lived here (Lena Christ; *Simplicissimus* artists Th. Th. Heine and Bruno Paul; landscape painter Philipp Röth). After incorporation in **1899**, the city reinforced the artistic identity by naming streets after painters — Tizian, Tintoretto, Tiepolo, Canaletto, Böcklin. Two celebrated interwar model estates followed: the **Borstei** (1924–29) and the GEWOFAG **Siedlung Neuhausen** (1928–31, master-planned by Hans Döllgast with Sep Ruf among others).

*Sources:* muenchen.de/stadtteile/neuhausen · abendzeitung-muenchen.de (Eingemeindung 1890; Kasernenviertel) · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Krauß · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotkreuzplatz · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Villenkolonie_Gern · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borstei · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siedlung_Neuhausen

# PART III — THE ROYAL LANDSCAPE: PALACE, CANAL, AND HUNT

## 10. Why Nymphenburg is here: a votive gift (1662–1664)

The whole Baroque overlay springs from a single dynastic event. In **1662**, after ten years of marriage, the Italian-born Electress **Henriette Adelaide of Savoy** and Elector **Ferdinand Maria** finally produced the long-awaited heir, **Max Emanuel**. In thanks, Ferdinand Maria gave his wife the dairy-estate **Schwaige Kemnat** northwest of Munich (bought 1663 for 10,000 florins), and in **1664** construction began of a five-storey cubic Italianate villa to plans by **Agostino Barelli** (architect of Munich's Theatine Church). The central pavilion was complete by **1675**.

The name was itself a self-mythologisation: Henriette Adelaide cast her domain as a **"borgo delle ninfe"** — the *castle of the nymphs* — understanding herself as a ruler blessed by the nymphs, patroness of a fertile Arcadia. On the park front, nymphs pay homage to **Flora**, goddess of blossoming. (Scholarly caution: the exact Italian phrase may be a 19th-century embellishment, but the nymph theme is original and pervasive.)

*Sources:* schloss-nymphenburg.de · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nymphenburg_Palace · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schloss_Nymphenburg · reise-zikaden.de · munich-heritage.de

## 11. The great canal network (1601–1730): a Baroque hydraulic masterpiece

The waterway running **east from the palace toward the city** is the famous axis, but it is only one limb of the **Nordmünchner Kanalsystem** ("North Munich canal system") — described as unique in Central Europe, originally ~**50–51 km**, of which ~**36 km still carry water** 300 years on. It was built to link and supply the three Wittelsbach suburban palaces — **Nymphenburg, Schleißheim and Dachau** — with water and water-borne transport. Its phases:

- **1601** — a first **Würmkanal** is dug to bring Würm water to the Schleißheim estate, establishing the principle of tapping the Würm.
- **1687–1692** — under **Max Emanuel**, the Schleißheim canals (architect Enrico Zuccalli), drawing on both the Würm and the Isar.
- **1701–1703** — the **Pasing–Nymphenburger Kanal** branches from the Würm at Pasing to feed the palace park (the river loses ~⅓ of its water to it). A documented social cost: farmers of Obermenzing and Pipping were forced to cede land without compensation, the work done in corvée labour.
- **1702–1704** — the **Nymphenburg–Biedersteiner Kanal**, built to *drain* surplus water away ~15 km northeast toward Schwabing and the English Garden. (Buried under the Schwabing freight rail-yard in 1901; re-opened in the late 1980s, creating the Schwabinger See.)
- **1728–1730** — under Elector **Karl Albrecht**, the **eastern canal toward the city**, flanked by the **Nördliche** and **Südliche Auffahrtsallee**. This was meant to be the central axis of an ambitious new ideal town, **"Karlstadt,"** which was **never built** — the canal and its avenues are its only lasting trace, and they pre-drew the street-grid of modern Neuhausen-Nymphenburg.

**Engineering.** Inside the park the water feeds the **Mittelkanal** sight-axis, the **Große Kaskade** (built 1717 by Joseph Effner, river-gods symbolising Isar and Danube), the two lakes, and — crucially — the **pump works** that drive the fountains by the Würm's natural gradient alone, with no external power. **Joseph von Baader's** installations (Grünes Brunnhaus, 1803; Johannisbrunnhaus, 1807/08), using his invented **Windkessel** air-pressure vessel, are celebrated as among the **oldest still-operating pump-works in Europe**.

**Use.** The canals carried **building-stone by barge** for the palace works; they hosted a **fleet of Venetian gondolas** (figures range from ~40 to ~80) with gondoliers brought from Venice, who lived where the Palm House now stands; Max Emanuel even built a three-storey ceremonial state-ship, the **Bucentaur**, on the Starnberger See. Court gondola rides ran **1703–1850**. The canals also powered mills and, to this day, the **Nymphenburg Porcelain Manufactory** (in the Rondell since 1761) by a water-driven belt system. An **Eisablasskanal** served **ice-harvesting**. In winter the frozen canal is still used for **ice-skating and Eisstockschießen** (Bavarian curling) — today between the Gerner Brücke and Hubertusstraße.

## 12. The park transformed: from Baroque parterre to English landscape

The garden began (1701–04) as a French-Baroque scheme by **Charles Carbonet**, interrupted when Max Emanuel went into exile in 1704. From c. 1715 **Dominique Girard** (trained in Le Nôtre's Versailles) and **Joseph Effner** realised the grand design — a rectangle of canals setting palace and parterre on an "island," the Great Cascade closing the west.

From **1799/1804–1823**, **Friedrich Ludwig von Sckell** reworked it into an **English landscape park** (now ~180 ha within the wall, ~229 ha total) — but conservatively: unlike Capability Brown in England, *Sckell preserved the Baroque parterre, the central canal axis and the cascade.* His features include the lakeside **Apollotempel** (final stone version by Leo von Klenze, 1862–65). The parterre's mythological statuary (Marchiori's Saturn and Cybele, 1765; Roman Anton Boos's Olympians, 1778–85) survives.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlosspark_Nymphenburg · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nymphenburg_Palace_Park · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Apollotempel · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Marchiori

## 13. The Hubertusbrunnen at the canal's eastern end

At the **eastern end of the palace canal** (Waisenhausstraße, near Romanplatz) stands the **Hubertusbrunnen**, a roofed temple-fountain by sculptor **Adolf von Hildebrand** and architect Carl Sattler, built **1903–07**. Inside, a bronze **stag bears a glowing cross between its antlers** — the conversion vision of **St. Hubert**, patron of hunters — crowned by a figure of the saint himself. Unveiled **18 June 1907** as the citizens' gift to **Prince Regent Luitpold** on his 85th birthday, it originally stood by the Bavarian National Museum. In **1937** the Nazi regime ordered it demolished to clear Prinzregentenstraße; the museum's directors instead dismantled it stone by numbered stone and stored it. Through the efforts of **Crown Prince Rupprecht** and a citizens' initiative it was **re-erected on the Nymphenburg canal in 1954**, re-dedicated on St. Hubert's feast day, 3 November.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubertusbrunnen · municharttogo.zikg.eu/items/show/98 · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Hubertusbrunnen

## 14. The Hirschgarten: a princely deer-park turned people's beer-garden

Just southeast of the palace lies the **Hirschgarten** ("Deer Garden"). Its ground saw a string of failed court enterprises — a **pheasantry from 1720**, **hops from 1767**, then **17,000 mulberry trees** for a failed silk venture in 1786. The decisive act came in **1780**, when Elector **Karl Theodor** had his Chief Master of the Hunt, **Johann Theodor Freiherr von Waldkirch**, fence off ~44.6 hectares and stock it with **about 100 fallow and red deer** as a noble hunting reserve, closed to the public.

That exclusivity lasted barely a decade. Against the backdrop of the **French Revolution (1789)**, the Enlightenment-minded Karl Theodor — the same elector who gave Munich the English Garden — opened the Hirschgarten to the citizens as a conciliatory "gift to the people." In **1791** the resident gamekeeper was licensed to run an inn in the new **Jägerhaus** — **the birth of the beer garden**. After the 1918 fall of the monarchy, ownership passed to the **Wittelsbacher Ausgleichsfonds** (1923), which still leases it — hence the surviving name *Königlicher Hirschgarten*. Today it is the **largest beer garden in Europe** (~8,000 seats), and — in an unbroken thread from 1780 — a **fenced enclosure of fallow deer and mouflon** still lives beside it.

This sat within a far older Wittelsbach hunting landscape: Max Emanuel's enormous **Hirschjagdpark** (laid out from 1715, ~50 km circumference, with star-shaped hunting rides and coloured wooden lodges — Yellow, Green, Red, Blue House), the Rococo hunting lodge **Amalienburg** (Cuvilliés, 1734–39) with its rooftop pheasant-shooting platform, an active **falconry** tradition (the noble quarry was the grey heron), and a court **menagerie**.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirschgarten_(München) · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirschjagdpark · en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amalienburg · waf-bayern.de · schloss-nymphenburg.de/deutsch/park/tiere.htm

# PART IV — FOLKLORE, LEGEND, AND GHOST-LORE

## 15. The Black Woman of the Wittelsbachs (*Die Schwarze Frau*)

**[LEGEND]** — Nymphenburg's principal ghost walks all the Wittelsbach palaces — the Residenz, Schleißheim, Nymphenburg, Aschaffenburg. She is the **Schwarze Frau**, a death-omen: **tall and slender, dressed wholly in black, a black veil about her head, an apple in her hand**, gliding silently while doors and locks open and shut of their own accord. Her appearance announces a death in the House of Wittelsbach.

The legend is buttressed by "confirmations": at a **costume ball on Shrove Tuesday 1864** an unknown woman in black mourning was seen gliding through a door — and within weeks **three Wittelsbachs were dead** (King Max II on 10 March, Archduchess Hildegarde on 2 April, Princess Luitpold on 26 April 1864). She was said to be seen again around the night of **12–13 June 1886**, when **King Ludwig II** drowned. Tradition splits on her identity: most often **Electress Maria Anna** (1728–97), who herself wrote that "in our family it is said that she wanders as the 'Black Woman'…"; or the founder of Nymphenburg, **Henriette Adelaide**, who saved her children in the 1674 Residenz fire and returns veiled in mourning. A companion tale in Schinzel-Penth's collection, *"Geheimnisvolle Schritte in Nymphenburg"* ("Mysterious Footsteps"), records phantom steps in the palace.

*Sources:* munichkindl.net/schwarze-frau · volkverlag.de · kohlrabenschwarz-fans.de · epub.ub.uni-muenchen.de/27294 (Detlef Willand, *Die schwarze Frau in der Residenz*) · stadtgeschichte-muenchen.de/literatur/inhaltsverzeichnis.php?id=353

## 16. The Magdalenenklause: a deliberate ruin and a grotto of penance

**[DOCUMENTED HISTORY + religious folklore]** — In a remote wooded corner of the park stands the **Magdalenenklause** (Magdalene Hermitage), built **1725–28** by Joseph Effner for the aging Max Emanuel — among the **first deliberate artificial ruins in European garden art**. From outside it mimics a crumbling ruin — **cracks and falling plaster built in on purpose** — a *memento mori* on "the transience of earthly things." The northern rooms are a bare monk's cell "à la Capucine"; the southern half is a **grotto chapel** encrusted with tuff, shells and coloured stones, holding a **stucco Mary Magdalene** (Volpini, 1726), the penitent saint weeping in her cave. It remains a living devotion: **pilgrims still visit on Magdalene's Day, 22 July**; the Klause recently marked its 300th anniversary.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magdalenenklause · schloesserblog.bayern.de · schloss-nymphenburg.de/deutsch/p-burgen/magdalen.htm · evangelische-zeitung.de

## 17. The Wasservogelfest: a pagan rain-rite on the palace canal

**[FOLKLORE / LIVING CUSTOM, with pagan roots]** — The strongest water-folklore of the area survives as the **Wasservogelfest** ("Water-Bird Festival") of Neuhausen and Moosach, whose climax happens **on the Nymphenburg canal at the Gerner Brücke**. Its roots, the sources agree, "likely trace back to heathen traditions": a young man was disguised as a **bird with reeds and leaves**, given a wooden bird's-head scepter, and **thrown into the water** — a **rain-magic / water-fertility rite** praying for "sufficient water for the summer months," observed around Pentecost.

In **1828** the Neuhausen procession set out to carry its water-bird to **King Ludwig I** at Nymphenburg — but the rival **Moosach** procession arrived first, a **violent brawl** erupted at the palace gates, and the scandalised king **banned the festival**. After 179 years it was **revived in 2007**, when over 4,000 people processed down Winthirstraße and flung the water-bird from the Gerner Brücke into the palace canal; it now runs biennially.

The surrounding marshland carried its own beliefs: in the Aubinger Lohe / Moosschwaige, local legend still speaks of a **White Woman, a "Devil's Mountain," "Fire Men,"** and **Irrlichter (will-o'-the-wisps)** — the marsh-lights long held to be nature-spirits' deceptions or the souls of the unquiet drowned, luring travellers into the bog.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wasservogelfest · muenchenwiki.de/wiki/Wasservogelfest · muenchen.de/veranstaltungen (Wasservogelfest) · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aubinger_Lohe · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrlicht

## 18. The St. Hubert stag and the Magdalenenfest

**[LEGEND + LIVING CUSTOM]** — The Hirschgarten's deer identity ties to the **Wunderhirsch** — the miraculous stag with a crucifix between its antlers, the conversion vision of **St. Hubert** (and, in older tradition, St. Eustace), embodied in the Hubertusbrunnen (§13). The grounds also host the **Magdalenenfest**, a Munich folk festival **documented since 1728** and held in the Hirschgarten since 1959 — a living thread to old Munich religious-festival tradition.

*Sources:* de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wunderhirsch · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubertus_von_Lüttich · de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hirschgarten_(München)

## 19. The birth of the Fairy-Tale King, and a court scandal

**[DOCUMENTED HISTORY with a legendary aura]** — **King Ludwig II was born at Nymphenburg on 25 August 1845**, and his birth already carried portents. His grandfather **King Ludwig I** had wished the child born in August to share his birthday — and recorded the famous **dispute of the clocks**: the boy arrived "after 20 minutes by the Nymphenburg clock, after 30 by my clock," whereupon the proud grandfather simply decreed his preferred time official. The date is the feast of **St. Louis IX of France**, ancestor of the Bourbons — a double royal-saint omen woven into the later mythology of the doomed king. The birth-room is still shown.

The palace's most famous human drama hangs in its south wing: King Ludwig I's **Schönheitengalerie (Gallery of Beauties)**, 36–38 portraits by Joseph Stieler (1827–50) of noblewomen and commoners alike — including the shoemaker's daughter **Helene Sedlmayr** ("the beautiful Munich girl") and the explosive **Lola Montez**, the adventuress whose hold over the king helped trigger the **Revolution of 1848** and his **abdication**.

**[ESOTERIC / MODERN OVERLAY]** — A genre of modern Munich "mystery" writing reads the park as a coded **Mysteriengarten**, with trees assigned to deities (oak–Jupiter, laurel–Apollo, linden–Venus) and a hidden "initiation path" of power-lines, culminating in the goat-god **Pan** seated on a spring beneath dark yews. This is best understood as a 20th–21st-century romantic overlay, not documented Baroque intent — but it is now part of Nymphenburg's living folklore. A related ghost-tale (Süßmuth's *111 Spukorte*) sets the conjuring of an alchemical **homunculus** by the Elector in the China-themed **Pagodenburg**.

# Open questions and caveats

- **Direct-fetch blocking (HTTP 403)** affected nearly every authoritative site in this environment; the factual backbone (dates, names, events) is corroborated across multiple independent sources, but verbatim quotation of a few legends (the Pagodenburg homunculus; the Nymphenburg phantom-footsteps) could not be retrieved in full and rests on bibliographic confirmation.
- **Winthir's dating** (8th vs. 12th century) is genuinely unresolved in the scholarship.
- **"Carl von Effner at Nymphenburg":** his role as royal garden director is documented, but a specific dated intervention by *Carl* (vs. his Baroque ancestor *Joseph*) Effner inside the park was not confirmed.
- **Unverified popular claims** flagged in the source streams: a Roman fort under Pasing's Würm-island castle; the term "Stelzen" for the canal barges; exact gondola counts (~40 vs. ~80); a dedicated Hammerwerk; and any specific named canal flood/drowning incident.
- For deeper primary work the key printed references are **Schöppner, *Sagenbuch der bayerischen Lande*** (free on Zeno.org / Projekt Gutenberg); **Schinzel-Penth, *Sagen und Legenden von München***; **Süßmuth, *111 Spukorte in und um München***; and the local **Geschichtswerkstatt Neuhausen** archive.
